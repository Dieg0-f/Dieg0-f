Key SVM Parameters to Tune

When using Support Vector Machines (SVM), several hyperparameters can significantly affect model performance. Here are the most important ones to consider:

a. Kernel Type (kernel)

  • 'linear': Best suited for linearly separable data.
  • 'rbf' (default): Radial Basis Function, effective for non-linear relationships.
  • 'poly': Polynomial kernel, introduces non-linearity through polynomial transformations.
  • 'sigmoid': Acts like a neural network activation function.

b. Regularization Parameter (C)

Controls the trade-off between a smooth decision boundary and correct classification of training examples.

  • High C:

    • Smaller margin
    • Tries to classify all training points correctly
    • Low bias, high variance

  • Low C:

    • Larger margin
    • Allows misclassification of some training points
    • High bias, low variance

c. Kernel Coefficient (gamma)

Defines how far the influence of a single training example reaches.

  • Low gamma:

    • Influence is far-reaching
    • Model is smoother and simpler
    • High bias

  • High gamma:

    • Influence is close
    • Model fits the training data more closely
    • High variance / overfitting risk

d. Epsilon (epsilon) used in SVR (Support Vector Regression)

Defines the margin of tolerance where no penalty is given for errors.

  • Small epsilon:

    • Model follows training data very closely
    • Potential for overfitting

e. Degree (degree) only for 'poly' kernel

Defines the degree of the polynomial function used.

  • Higher degree:

    • More flexible decision boundary
    • Increased risk of overfitting

from sklearn.model_selection import GridSearchCV

# Define parameter grid
param_grid = {
    'C': [0.1, 1, 10, 100],
    'gamma': [0.01, 0.1, 1, 10],
    'kernel': ['rbf', 'linear'],
    'epsilon': [0.1, 0.5, 1]

Support Vectors Interpretations

There are 62 support vectors, each one with 14 features. This means that there are 62 data points defining the decision boundary of your SVM model. The more support vectors there are, the more complex and detailed your model is. However, be cautious, as having too many support vectors may indicate that the model is overfitting.
